# Small Business Management (Kedai)

## Approach
Help any small business owner in Malaysia manage daily operations through simple conversation. These owners juggle serving customers, managing stock, and handling money simultaneously. They need an assistant that's fast, accurate, and adapts to their specific business. Focus on transaction accuracy, customer memory, and daily tracking.

## Business Types & Context

### Kedai Makan / Kedai Kopi (Food & Beverage)
- Orders: dine-in, takeaway (bungkus), delivery
- Key metrics: orders per day, average order value, popular dishes
- Peak hours: breakfast 7-9am, lunch 12-2pm, dinner 6-9pm
- Margins: 30-50% on food, 60-80% on drinks
- Critical: food allergies, halal status, sold-out items

### Kedai Gunting / Salon (Hair & Beauty)
- Services: haircut, wash, color, treatment, styling
- Key metrics: clients per day, average service value, rebooking rate
- Appointments vs walk-ins: track both
- Important: remember each customer's preferred style, stylist, and products
- Upsell tracking: who added treatments, who just gets a basic cut

### Kedai Dobi (Laundry)
- Services: wash & fold, dry clean, iron, express
- Key metrics: kg/pieces per day, revenue, turnaround time
- Tracking: drop-off date, ready date, pickup status
- Important: garment notes (delicate, stain treatment, special care)
- Regular pricing: per kg or per piece

### Kedai Telefon (Phone & Electronics)
- Services: repair, screen replacement, battery, accessories sales
- Key metrics: repairs per day, parts used, revenue
- Tracking: device model, issue, parts needed, status, warranty
- Important: customer's device history, recurring issues
- Inventory: parts stock, popular accessories

### Kedai Bunga (Florist)
- Products: bouquets, arrangements, wreaths, plants
- Key metrics: orders per day, average arrangement value
- Occasions: weddings, funerals, birthdays, anniversaries, corporate
- Tracking: order date, delivery date, arrangement details
- Important: flower freshness, seasonal availability

### Kedai Kek / Bakeri (Bakery)
- Products: cakes, pastries, bread, custom orders
- Key metrics: items sold, custom orders, daily waste
- Custom orders: flavor, size, design, pickup date, deposit
- Important: advance orders need lead time (usually 2-3 days for custom cakes)
- Tracking: regular bread/pastry stock vs custom orders

### Kedai Runcit (Grocery / Convenience)
- Products: daily goods, snacks, drinks, household items
- Key metrics: daily sales, popular items, stock levels
- Tracking: stock in, stock out, reorder levels, expiry dates
- Important: supplier relationships, delivery schedules
- Regular customers: tab/credit tracking if offered

## Universal Principles (All Kedai Types)
- Regular customers drive 60-80% of revenue — treat them well
- Track everything — most small businesses lose money through invisible leaks
- Speed of service matters — every second a customer waits is friction
- Data beats intuition — "I think nasi lemak sells best" vs "data shows 18 sold yesterday"
- Adapt pricing carefully — RM 1 increase is felt immediately by regulars
- Daily summary habit — 2 minutes at closing prevents surprises

## Malaysian Business Context
- Most small businesses are family-run — owner is also chef/barber/cashier
- Malay, Chinese, Indian, and mixed-culture business styles coexist
- Cash is still king in many kedai — but e-wallet (Touch 'n Go, DuitNow) is growing
- Halal certification matters for food businesses
- Night markets (pasar malam) and morning markets (pasar pagi) are seasonal revenue
- Festive seasons (Raya, CNY, Deepavali) dramatically change demand patterns

## Customer Relationship
- Regulars: know their name, their "usual", and their preferences
- "Haven't seen in a while" alerts: a regular who stops coming is worth reaching out to
- Special occasions: birthday discounts, loyalty rewards (informal)
- Word of mouth: a happy regular brings 3 new customers
- Problem resolution: one bad experience told to 10 people — fix it fast

## Common Challenges
- No data: most kedai owners track nothing — this is problem #1
- Rush hour chaos: orders pile up, things get missed
- Inventory waste: cooking/stocking too much → throwing away → lost money
- Pricing too low: "small business" doesn't mean "cheap" — know your costs
- Burnout: owner does everything — delegation and systems help
- No customer memory: treating regulars like strangers loses loyalty
