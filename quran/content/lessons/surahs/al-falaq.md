# Surah Al-Falaq — The Daybreak

The first of the two "refuge surahs" (al-Mu'awwidhatain). The Prophet (peace be upon him) recited these before sleep and after each prayer. Al-Falaq seeks refuge from external evils in the created world.

## Verse 1
قُلْ أَعُوذُ بِرَبِّ الْفَلَقِ

*Qul a'udhu bi-Rabbil falaq*
Say: I seek refuge with the Lord of the daybreak.

**Key Words:**
- قُلْ (qul) → say — root ق-و-ل (to say/speak). Command to the Prophet and to every reciter
- أَعُوذُ (a'udhu) → I seek refuge — root ع-و-ذ (to take refuge, to seek protection). This is the root of ta'awwudh — the act of seeking refuge in Allah before reciting Quran
- بِرَبِّ (bi-Rabb) → with the Lord of — root ر-ب-ب (to nurture, to sustain). Rabb emphasizes Allah as the one who owns, controls, and protects
- الْفَلَقِ (al-falaq) → the daybreak/the splitting dawn — root ف-ل-ق (to split, to cleave open). Falaq is the moment darkness splits open and light breaks through

**Root Spotlight:** ف-ل-ق (f-l-q) = to split. The dawn "splits" the darkness. The same root describes how Allah splits a seed to bring forth a plant (6:95). Seeking refuge with the Lord of the daybreak reminds us that all light — physical and spiritual — comes from Allah.

## Verse 2
مِن شَرِّ مَا خَلَقَ

*Min sharri ma khalaqa*
From the evil of what He created.

**Key Words:**
- مِن (min) → from — preposition indicating source of protection
- شَرِّ (sharr) → evil/harm — root ش-ر-ر (to be evil, to be harmful). Sharr is the opposite of khayr (good). This is a broad, general refuge from all created evil
- مَا (ma) → what/that which — relative pronoun encompassing everything
- خَلَقَ (khalaqa) → He created — root خ-ل-ق (to create). Khalq = creation. This is the same root as Khaliq (the Creator), one of Allah's names

**Root Spotlight:** خ-ل-ق (kh-l-q) = to create, to shape. Allah is al-Khaliq (the Creator). Everything that was created — including evil forces — is under His control. Seeking refuge with the Creator from His creation affirms His sovereignty.

## Verse 3
وَمِن شَرِّ غَاسِقٍ إِذَا وَقَبَ

*Wa min sharri ghāsiqin idhā waqab*
And from the evil of the darkness when it settles.

**Key Words:**
- غَاسِقٍ (ghasiq) → darkness/the dark night — root غ-س-ق (to be intensely dark, to overflow into darkness). Refers to the darkness of night when evil forces are most active
- إِذَا (idha) → when — conditional time marker
- وَقَبَ (waqaba) → it settles/it intensifies/it covers — root و-ق-ب (to penetrate, to enter, to settle in). The darkness "enters" and covers everything

**Root Spotlight:** غ-س-ق (gh-s-q) = deep darkness. Ghasiq describes the dense darkness of night. Some classical scholars also interpreted this as a reference to the moon when it wanes or eclipses — the moon "penetrating" the night. Either way, it represents times of hidden dangers.

## Verse 4
وَمِن شَرِّ النَّفَّاثَاتِ فِي الْعُقَدِ

*Wa min sharrin-naffathati fil 'uqad*
And from the evil of those who blow into knots.

**Key Words:**
- النَّفَّاثَاتِ (an-naffathat) → those who blow — root ن-ف-ث (to blow with the mouth, to spit lightly). Naffathat is an intensive feminine plural: those who repeatedly blow. A reference to practitioners of black magic who blow over tied knots
- فِي (fi) → into/in — preposition
- الْعُقَدِ ('uqad) → the knots — root ع-ق-د (to tie, to make a knot/contract). 'Aqd = a knot or a contract. The plural 'uqad refers to the knotted cords used in certain forms of witchcraft

**Root Spotlight:** ع-ق-د ('a-q-d) = to tie/bind. A marriage contract is an 'aqd. A knot is an 'uqda. The same word used for binding agreements is used here for binding magic — evil that attempts to restrict and harm through binding.

## Verse 5
وَمِن شَرِّ حَاسِدٍ إِذَا حَسَدَ

*Wa min sharri hasidin idha hasad*
And from the evil of an envier when he envies.

**Key Words:**
- حَاسِدٍ (hasid) → an envier/one who envies — root ح-س-د (to envy, to begrudge). Hasid is the active participle: a person currently characterized by envy
- إِذَا (idha) → when — emphasizes the moment envy becomes active
- حَسَدَ (hasada) → he envies/when he acts on envy — same root ح-س-د, past tense verb. The repetition (hasid...hasada) intensifies: we seek refuge specifically when the envier actively directs their envy

**Root Spotlight:** ح-س-د (h-s-d) = envy, begrudging another's blessing. Hasad is specifically the wish that someone's blessing be removed — distinct from ghibtah (wishing for the same blessing without begrudging). The surah ends with this evil because hasad can be among the most spiritually and practically destructive forces.

## Summary
Al-Falaq is a surah of refuge from four categories of evil: all created evil in general, the dangers of night and darkness, those who use magic and witchcraft, and the destructive force of envy. The key verb is أَعُوذُ — "I seek refuge" — establishing that protection comes only from Allah (Rabb al-falaq).
