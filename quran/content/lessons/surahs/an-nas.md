# Surah An-Nas — Mankind

The final surah of the Quran and the second of the two "refuge surahs" (al-Mu'awwidhatain). Where Al-Falaq seeks refuge from external physical evils, An-Nas seeks refuge from an internal, invisible one: the whisperer who attacks from within the human heart.

## Verse 1
قُلْ أَعُوذُ بِرَبِّ النَّاسِ

*Qul a'udhu bi-Rabbin-nas*
Say: I seek refuge with the Lord of mankind.

**Key Words:**
- قُلْ (qul) → say — root ق-و-ل (to say/speak). The same command that opens Al-Ikhlas and Al-Falaq — a recurring instruction directing the believer to speak these words consciously
- أَعُوذُ (a'udhu) → I seek refuge — root ع-و-ذ (to take refuge, to seek shelter). The central act of this surah and Al-Falaq
- بِرَبِّ (bi-Rabb) → with the Lord of — root ر-ب-ب (to nurture, sustain, have authority over)
- النَّاسِ (an-nas) → mankind/people — root ن-و-س or أ-ن-س (to be sociable, to be familiar). Nas refers to all of humanity collectively. It appears in every verse of this surah, forming the surah's distinctive ring structure

**Root Spotlight:** ن-و-س / أ-ن-س (n-w-s / '-n-s) = people, humans, familiarity. Nas (people) and ins (humans, as opposed to jinn) come from related roots about sociability and familiarness. The word appears in the title and in all 6 verses, making it the most repeated word in this surah.

## Verse 2
مَلِكِ النَّاسِ

*Malikin-nas*
The King of mankind.

**Key Words:**
- مَلِكِ (Malik) → King — root م-ل-ك (to possess, to have dominion). Malik = the one who owns and rules. Compare with Maalik (Owner) in Al-Fatihah — Malik emphasizes sovereign authority while Maalik emphasizes ownership
- النَّاسِ (an-nas) → mankind — repeated to reinforce that all people are under Allah's sovereignty, not just believers

**Root Spotlight:** م-ل-ك (m-l-k) = kingship, dominion, ownership. Mulk = kingdom. Malak = angel (one who carries out the King's commands). Milk = property. This is one of the most productive roots in Arabic. In this surah, Allah is declared King before being declared God — authority precedes worship.

## Verse 3
إِلَٰهِ النَّاسِ

*Ilahin-nas*
The God of mankind.

**Key Words:**
- إِلَٰهِ (Ilah) → God/deity — root أ-ل-ه (to be worshipped, to be divine). Ilah is the generic word for a deity — the one who deserves worship. Allah is the proper name; Ilah is the title. The shahada says "La ilaha illa Allah" — no deity worthy of worship except Allah
- النَّاسِ (an-nas) → mankind — third repetition. Allah is declared Lord, King, and God of all people in three consecutive verses

**Root Spotlight:** أ-ل-ه ('-l-h) = divinity, worship, the state of being deserving of worship. Uluhiyyah = the quality of being God. This verse completes a triple declaration: Rabb (nurturing Lord), Malik (sovereign King), and Ilah (worthy of worship) — three different dimensions of Allah's relationship to humanity.

## Verse 4
مِن شَرِّ الْوَسْوَاسِ الْخَنَّاسِ

*Min sharril-waswasil-khannas*
From the evil of the retreating whisperer.

**Key Words:**
- شَرِّ (sharr) → evil — root ش-ر-ر (to be evil, harmful). Same word as in Al-Falaq — the evil sought protection from
- الْوَسْوَاسِ (al-waswas) → the whisperer/the one who whispers — root و-س-و-س (to whisper, to make a rustling sound). Waswas is a repetitive, echoing sound — like the rustle of leaves or a persistent whisper. Shaitan's method is not loud commands but subtle, repeated suggestions
- الْخَنَّاسِ (al-khannas) → the one who retreats/withdraws — root خ-ن-س (to retreat, to shrink back, to hide). Khannas is intensive: one who constantly retreats. Shaitan retreats when Allah is remembered (dhikr) and returns when remembrance stops

**Root Spotlight:** و-س-و-س (w-s-w-s) = to whisper repeatedly. Waswasah = whispers, doubts. This root perfectly captures how Shaytan works — not through force, but through persistent, barely-perceptible suggestions that compound over time.

**Root Spotlight:** خ-ن-س (kh-n-s) = to retreat, shrink back, hide. The name Khannas tells us the antidote: remembrance of Allah causes Shaitan to retreat. This is why the surah is recited — the very act of seeking refuge drives back the whisperer.

## Verse 5
الَّذِي يُوَسْوِسُ فِي صُدُورِ النَّاسِ

*Alladhi yuwaswisu fi sudorin-nas*
Who whispers into the chests of mankind.

**Key Words:**
- الَّذِي (alladhi) → who/that which — relative pronoun identifying what al-khannas does
- يُوَسْوِسُ (yuwaswisu) → he whispers — present tense verb from root و-س-و-س. The present tense indicates this is ongoing, continuous activity — happening right now, all the time
- فِي (fi) → into/within — the whispering goes inside
- صُدُورِ (sudur) → chests/hearts — root ص-د-ر (the chest, the front, the place of forward movement). Sudur is the plural of sadr (chest). In Quranic usage, the sadr is the seat of the heart and emotions — where thoughts, feelings, and intentions originate

**Root Spotlight:** ص-د-ر (s-d-r) = chest, front, to issue from. Sadr = chest (the physical place where the heart sits). Verse 5 tells us where Shaitan's attack lands: not in the body, but in the innermost seat of human consciousness and will.

## Verse 6
مِنَ الْجِنَّةِ وَالنَّاسِ

*Minal-jinnati wan-nas*
From among the jinn and mankind.

**Key Words:**
- مِنَ (mina) → from among — partitive preposition indicating the whisperer can be from either category
- الْجِنَّةِ (al-jinnah) → the jinn — root ج-ن-ن (to be hidden, to be concealed). Jinn are hidden beings; the word shares a root with jannah (paradise — the hidden/enclosed garden) and janin (fetus — the hidden one). Jinn can be believers or disbelievers; here the reference is to those among the jinn who whisper evil
- وَالنَّاسِ (wan-nas) → and mankind — final appearance of nas. The surah closes by revealing that whisperers can be human beings too, not only Shaytan

**Root Spotlight:** ج-ن-ن (j-n-n) = to be hidden, concealed. Jinnah = jinn (hidden beings). Jannah = paradise (the enclosed, lush garden). Janin = fetus. Junun = madness (a state where reason is "hidden"). This verse is significant: the threat of waswas (whispering) is not only supernatural — humans who plant doubts, spread rumors, and corrupt others are also "whisperers."

## Summary
An-Nas closes the Quran with the most intimate battle: the struggle within the human heart. The surah's structure is remarkable — nas (mankind) appears in every verse, and Allah is introduced with three titles before the enemy is even named. The order matters: know your Rabb, your Malik, your Ilah — then face the whisperer. The antidote to waswas is already embedded in the surah: conscious remembrance of Allah (Rabb, Malik, Ilah of all people) causes al-khannas to retreat.
