# Lesson 10: Messengers, Angels, and Moral Qualities

## Words

- مَلَك (malak) → angel — appears ~88 times
  Ayah: "وَالْمَلَائِكَةُ يَدْخُلُونَ عَلَيْهِم مِّن كُلِّ بَابٍ" — "And the angels will enter upon them from every gate." (13:23)

- نَبِيّ (nabi) → prophet (one who receives and conveys divine news) — appears ~75 times
  Ayah: "مَا كَانَ مُحَمَّدٌ أَبَا أَحَدٍ مِّن رِّجَالِكُمْ وَلَٰكِن رَّسُولَ اللَّهِ وَخَاتَمَ النَّبِيِّينَ" — "Muhammad is not the father of any of your men, but he is the Messenger of Allah and the seal of the prophets." (33:40)

- رَسُول (rasul) → messenger / one who is sent — appears ~332 times
  Ayah: "مُّحَمَّدٌ رَّسُولُ اللَّهِ" — "Muhammad is the Messenger of Allah." (48:29)

- ظُلْم (dhulm) → injustice / wrongdoing / oppression / darkness — appears ~316 times (root occurrences)
  Ayah: "إِنَّ الشِّرْكَ لَظُلْمٌ عَظِيمٌ" — "Indeed, associating partners with Allah is a grave injustice." (31:13)

- صَبْر (sabr) → patience / steadfast endurance — appears ~90 times
  Ayah: "إِنَّمَا يُوَفَّى الصَّابِرُونَ أَجْرَهُم بِغَيْرِ حِسَابٍ" — "Indeed, the patient will be given their reward without account." (39:10)

## Root Connection

- **نَبِيّ** (root: ن-ب-أ, n-b-') → نَبَأ (news/information), أَنْبَأ (he informed). A نَبِيّ is one who brings divine news. **رَسُول** (root: ر-س-ل, r-s-l) → أَرْسَلَ (he sent), رِسَالَة (message). The distinction: every رَسُول is also a نَبِيّ, but not every نَبِيّ is a رَسُول (messengers bring a new scripture/law).
- **مَلَك** (root: م-ل-ك, m-l-k) shares its root with مَلِك (king) and مُلْك (sovereignty/kingdom). Angels are perhaps called مَلَائِكَة because they are "possessors" of divine command.
- **ظُلْم** (root: ظ-ل-م, dh-l-m) literally means to put something in the wrong place, and also means darkness. ظَالِم (oppressor), ظُلُمَات (darknesses — plural). The Quran frames injustice and spiritual darkness with the same root.
- **صَبْر** (root: ص-ب-ر, s-b-r) → صَابِر (patient one), اِصْبِرْ (be patient! — imperative), صَبُور (very patient — a divine name). The Quran commands صَبْر in over 90 places — it is the defining virtue of the believer under trial.

## Practice

1. What is the theological distinction between نَبِيّ and رَسُول? Use (33:40) to explain why the Quran uses both terms for Muhammad.
2. ظُلْم in (31:13) is described as "grave injustice." Who speaks this line, and to whom? Look up the full context of Luqman's advice.
3. صَبْر is promised reward "without account" in (39:10). What does this suggest about the value of patience in Quranic ethics?

## Reflection

> "وَلَنَبْلُوَنَّكُم بِشَيْءٍ مِّنَ الْخَوْفِ وَالْجُوعِ وَنَقْصٍ مِّنَ الْأَمْوَالِ وَالْأَنفُسِ وَالثَّمَرَاتِ وَبَشِّرِ الصَّابِرِينَ"
> "And We will surely test you with something of fear and hunger and a loss of wealth and lives and fruits, but give good tidings to the patient." (2:155)

الصَّابِرِينَ (the patient ones) is the plural active participle of صَبْر. This verse sits at the heart of the Quran's teaching on trial — and the answer to every form of ظُلْم is صَبْر.
