# Personal Finance Management

## Approach
Help people build financial awareness through simple tracking and honest feedback. Most people don't have a money problem — they have a visibility problem. They don't know where their money goes. Make it effortless to track, easy to understand, and rewarding to improve. No financial jargon. No guilt. Just clarity.

## Core Concepts

### The Visibility Principle
You can't manage what you can't see. The single most impactful financial habit is tracking spending. Not budgeting, not investing, not side hustles — just knowing where every ringgit goes.

### The 50/30/20 Rule (Starting Point)
- 50% Needs: rent, food, utilities, transport, insurance, loan payments
- 30% Wants: entertainment, dining out, shopping, hobbies, subscriptions
- 20% Savings: emergency fund, goals, investments, debt repayment above minimum
- This is a guideline, not a law. Adjust based on reality.

### Emergency Fund Priority
Before any other savings goal: build 3-6 months of expenses as emergency fund.
- 1 month = good start
- 3 months = basic safety net
- 6 months = solid financial security
- This protects against job loss, medical emergencies, car breakdowns

## Expense Categories
- **Food:** groceries + eating out (tracking separately is ideal)
- **Transport:** fuel, parking, tolls, public transport, ride-hailing
- **Housing:** rent/mortgage, maintenance, furnishing
- **Utilities:** electricity, water, internet, phone
- **Shopping:** clothes, electronics, household items
- **Entertainment:** streaming, games, movies, events, hobbies
- **Health:** medical, dental, pharmacy, gym, supplements
- **Education:** courses, books, tools, certifications
- **Debt:** loan repayments, credit card payments
- **Savings:** emergency fund, goals, investments
- **Other:** gifts, donations, miscellaneous

## Spending Patterns to Watch
- Weekend spending spikes — eating out, shopping, entertainment cluster on weekends
- Subscription creep — small monthly charges add up (RM 15 x 10 subs = RM 150/month)
- Lifestyle inflation — spending increases immediately after income increases
- Emotional spending — stress/boredom triggers unplanned purchases
- Convenience premium — grab rides, food delivery, pre-made meals cost 2-3x more

## Budgeting Strategies
- **Zero-based budget:** every ringgit has a job (income - all allocations = 0)
- **Envelope method:** allocate cash/virtual amounts per category, stop when empty
- **Pay yourself first:** auto-transfer savings on payday before spending
- **Anti-budget:** save a fixed amount, spend the rest guilt-free
- For beginners: start with anti-budget (simplest), graduate to zero-based

## Malaysian Context
- EPF (KWSP): mandatory savings for employed — already a form of retirement saving
- PTPTN: student loan — minimum RM 5/month payments, consider paying more
- ASB/ASM: government investment funds — low risk, decent returns for Bumiputera
- Medical card: important in Malaysia — company insurance vs personal
- Ringgit benchmarks: RM 2,500 is minimum wage, RM 4,000-6,000 is median income in KL

## Savings Milestones
- RM 100 saved — "First hundred! The habit is forming."
- RM 500 saved — "That covers a minor emergency."
- RM 1,000 saved — "Four digits! You're building real savings."
- RM 5,000 saved — "That's a solid emergency buffer."
- RM 10,000 saved — "Five figures. You're in control of your money."
- RM 50,000 saved — "This is life-changing money. Well done."

## Common Mistakes
- Not tracking at all — "I'll start next month" (you won't)
- Too-strict budgets — cutting all fun guarantees failure
- Ignoring small expenses — RM 5 coffee x 30 days = RM 150/month
- No emergency fund — one unexpected expense destroys the plan
- Comparing to others — someone else's spending is irrelevant to your situation
- Waiting to earn more before saving — save the percentage, not the amount

## Anti-patterns
- Don't guilt-trip about spending — show data, let the user decide
- Don't recommend specific investments or financial products
- Don't assume income level — ask, don't guess
- Don't push extreme frugality — sustainable beats aggressive
- Don't ignore the emotional side of money — spending has feelings attached to it
