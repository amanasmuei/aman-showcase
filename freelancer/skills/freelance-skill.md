# Freelance Business Management

## Approach
Help freelancers run their business with confidence. Most freelancers are great at their craft but struggle with the business side — invoicing, follow-ups, pricing, client management. Be the organized business partner they need. Focus on cash flow, clear communication, and sustainable practices.

## Client Management
- Always maintain a client profile: name, company, contact, rate, payment terms, communication preferences
- Categorize clients: active (current projects), recurring (regular work), dormant (no projects in 60+ days), problematic (late payments, scope creep)
- Red flags to watch: consistently late payments, expanding scope without budget increase, disrespectful communication
- Green flags to note: pays on time, clear communication, respects boundaries, provides referrals

## Pricing Strategy
- Hourly rates: good for uncertain scope, consulting, ongoing support
- Project-based: good for defined scope, creative work, deliverable-focused
- Retainer: good for recurring clients, predictable income, long-term relationships
- Value-based: charge for the value you deliver, not the time it takes
- Rate benchmarks: research market rates, factor in experience, don't race to the bottom
- Annual rate review: increase 5-15% annually based on experience gained and demand

## Invoice Best Practices
- Include: invoice number, date, client details, description of work, amount, payment terms, bank/payment details
- Payment terms: Net 15 for small projects, Net 30 standard, Net 45 only for enterprise clients
- Late payment protocol: reminder at due date, follow-up at 7 days, formal notice at 14 days, consider collections at 60+ days
- Always number invoices sequentially (INV-001, INV-002...) for tracking
- Keep records of all sent invoices and payment dates

## Communication Templates
- Proposal: hook (understanding of need), scope, timeline, deliverables, investment, next steps
- Follow-up (no response): "Just checking in on [proposal/project]. Happy to answer any questions."
- Invoice reminder (friendly): "Quick reminder that invoice #X for [amount] is due on [date]. Let me know if you need anything."
- Invoice reminder (firm): "Following up on invoice #X ([amount]), now [N] days past due. Please arrange payment at your earliest convenience."
- Rate increase: "Effective [date], my rate will be [new rate]. This reflects [reason: expanded skills, market adjustment, increased demand]. Current projects will continue at the agreed rate."
- Scope creep response: "Happy to include [new request]. This falls outside our original scope, so I'll send an updated quote for the additional work."

## Cash Flow Management
- Aim for 3 months of expenses in savings (runway buffer)
- Diversify: don't let one client be more than 40% of income
- Invoice immediately upon completion — don't delay
- Require deposits for new clients (50%) and large projects
- Track: total invoiced, total received, total outstanding, average days to payment

## Common Mistakes
- Undercharging: not accounting for taxes, expenses, non-billable time, health insurance
- Scope creep: doing extra work without adjusting the price
- Late invoicing: waiting weeks to send invoices (delays payment by the same amount)
- No contracts: working without written agreements leads to disputes
- Client concentration: relying too heavily on one client is risky
- Not tracking time: even project-based freelancers should know how long things take

## Level Guidelines
- Lv.1 (Starting): First clients, building portfolio, learning to price. Focus on getting work and delivering quality.
- Lv.2 (Established): Regular clients, consistent income. Focus on processes, rates, and client management.
- Lv.3 (Growing): Full pipeline, raising rates. Focus on efficiency, subcontracting, and scaling.
- Lv.4 (Senior): Premium rates, selective about clients. Focus on value-based pricing and passive income.
- Lv.5 (Agency): Managing a team, multiple concurrent projects. Focus on delegation and systems.
