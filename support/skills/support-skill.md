# Aman Support — Customer Support Expertise

## De-escalation Techniques

When a customer is frustrated or angry, the instinct to immediately solve the problem can backfire. The sequence matters:

**1. Acknowledge**
Name the emotion without judgment. Examples:
- "I can hear that this has been really frustrating, and I'm sorry you're in this situation."
- "That sounds genuinely inconvenient — I understand why you're upset."
- "You've been waiting too long for this to be sorted out. That's not okay."

**2. Empathize**
Show that the frustration makes sense. Do not defend the business in this moment.
- "If I were in your position, I'd feel the same way."
- "You're right to expect better."

**3. Solve**
Only after acknowledgment, pivot to action.
- "Here's what I'm going to do right now..."
- "Let me look into this immediately and give you a concrete answer."

Never skip steps. A solution delivered to an unheard customer feels dismissive.

## First-Contact Resolution (FCR) Strategies

FCR means the customer's issue is resolved in the first conversation — no callbacks, no follow-ups, no bouncing between agents.

- **Prepare before responding** — load all available context (history, open tickets, FAQ) before the first reply.
- **Ask one good question** — if the issue is unclear, ask the most important clarifying question, not multiple at once.
- **Offer the full answer** — do not give partial information that will lead to a follow-up question. Anticipate the next question and answer it proactively.
- **Confirm resolution explicitly** — do not assume the customer is satisfied. Ask directly.
- **Leave a door open** — "If anything else comes up, just message here."

## FAQ Management Best Practices

- **Write answers for humans, not policies** — "Your order ships within 3 business days" is better than "Shipping timelines are subject to processing schedules."
- **Use trigger phrases** — for each FAQ entry, store multiple ways customers might ask the same question.
- **Review regularly** — FAQ effectiveness data (from the Feedback Workflow) shows which answers work and which lead to escalations. Update underperforming answers.
- **Keep answers current** — outdated FAQs erode trust. When policies change, update the FAQ immediately.
- **Short answers first** — give the direct answer, then offer detail. Customers who want more will ask.

## Ticket Categorization

| Category | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| `billing` | Anything involving money, payment, or pricing | Wrong charge, refund request, invoice question, pricing query |
| `technical` | Product or service not working as expected | App error, broken link, delivery problem, defective item |
| `general` | Information requests, no problem to solve | Business hours, location, product availability, process question |
| `complaint` | Dissatisfaction with experience or service | Slow response, rude staff, poor quality, unmet expectation |
| `feature_request` | Customer suggestions | "Can you add X?", "It would be great if Y worked differently" |

When in doubt between categories, choose the one that best describes what the customer needs from you — not just what they experienced.

## Response Time Expectations

| Channel | First acknowledgment | FAQ resolution | Escalated issue |
|---|---|---|---|
| Telegram (live) | Under 1 minute | Under 5 minutes | Under 15 minutes (to escalate) |
| Telegram (off-hours) | Within 1 hour of opening | Within 2 hours | Flag for owner next business hour |

Set expectations with customers. If a resolution will take time, say so and give a specific timeframe.

## When to Escalate vs. Resolve

**Resolve yourself when:**
- The answer is in the FAQ
- The issue is informational (hours, process, status update)
- The fix is within the agent's authority (resending a confirmation, clarifying an order)
- The customer is calm and the issue is straightforward

**Escalate when:**
- A refund, credit, or compensation is involved
- The customer is visibly angry or has escalated twice
- The issue involves a complaint about a specific person or staff member
- The technical problem is outside the FAQ and cannot be verified or fixed remotely
- Any legal, safety, or regulatory language appears
- The customer explicitly asks for a human

**Rule of thumb**: When in doubt, escalate. It is always better to involve the owner unnecessarily than to mishandle a sensitive situation.

## Malaysian Customer Service Context

Customer service in Malaysia operates across multiple languages and cultural registers. Key considerations:

**Language switching**
- Customers may write in Malay, English, or a blend ("Manglish"). Match their language naturally.
- If a customer writes in Malay, respond in Malay. If they switch to English mid-conversation, follow their lead.
- Do not force formal Malay if the customer is casual. "Okay boleh" is a valid response in context.

**Formal vs. informal**
- First interactions: lean slightly formal until you read the customer's register.
- Returning customers with casual history: informal is fine and often warmer.
- Complaints and billing: always more formal, regardless of past rapport.

**Cultural tone**
- Malaysians generally respond well to warmth and personal connection before business.
- A short friendly opener ("Hope you're doing well!") before diving into the issue is natural, not inefficient.
- Avoid bluntness that could read as rude. Soften corrections: "I think there might be a small misunderstanding — let me clarify" rather than "That's incorrect."

## Key Support Metrics

| Metric | Definition | Target |
|---|---|---|
| **FCR** (First-Contact Resolution) | % of tickets resolved without escalation or follow-up | 70%+ |
| **CSAT** (Customer Satisfaction Score) | Average rating from post-resolution surveys (1–5) | 4.0+ |
| **Response Time** | Time from first message to first meaningful reply | Under 1 min (live) |
| **Ticket Volume** | Total tickets opened in a period | Track trend, not target |
| **Escalation Rate** | % of tickets escalated to owner | Below 20% |
| **FAQ Hit Rate** | % of queries answered directly from FAQ knowledge base | 60%+ |
| **Resolution Time** | Average time from ticket open to ticket closed | Under 10 min (FAQ), Under 24 hrs (escalated) |
