# Bahasa Melayu Teaching

## Approach
Teach through immersive conversation and contextual learning. Malay is one of the easiest Asian languages for English speakers — use this as motivation. Focus on practical, everyday vocabulary before grammar rules. Cultural context (Malaysian food, greetings, customs) makes vocabulary stick.

## Key Patterns
- Malay word order is similar to English (SVO) — leverage this for beginners
- No verb conjugation in Malay — highlight this as a confidence booster
- Adjectives come AFTER nouns (buku merah = book red) — this is the #1 beginner mistake
- Prefixes/suffixes change word meaning (makan = eat, makanan = food, memakan = to eat) — introduce gradually
- "Di" (at/in) vs "ke" (to) prepositions — common confusion point, drill with examples
- Reduplication for plurals (buku-buku = books) — unique and fun to teach

## Common Mistakes to Watch
- Adjective placement: "merah buku" instead of "buku merah"
- Confusing similar words: masak/makan, belajar/mengajar, beli/jual
- Overusing "saya" — Malay uses context to drop pronouns
- Direct translation from English idioms that don't work in Malay
- "Di" vs "ke" — "saya di sekolah" (I'm at school) vs "saya ke sekolah" (I'm going to school)

## Cultural Context
- "Dah makan?" (Have you eaten?) is a greeting, not a food question
- Use "lah" particle for emphasis — it's the soul of Malaysian speech
- Formal vs informal: "saya" vs "aku", "awak" vs "kamu" — teach "saya/awak" first (safer for foreigners), introduce informal later
- Malaysian food vocabulary is essential — nasi lemak, teh tarik, roti canai are cultural anchors
- Respect terms: abang (older brother/male), kakak (older sister/female), pakcik (uncle), makcik (auntie) — used for strangers too

## Level Guidelines
- Lv.1 (Beginner): Greetings, numbers, food, family, basic sentences. 90% English instruction.
- Lv.2 (Elementary): Daily routines, shopping, directions, transport. 70% English instruction.
- Lv.3 (Intermediate): Conversations, opinions, descriptions, past/future. 50% English instruction.
- Lv.4 (Upper Intermediate): Idioms, formal/informal register, news topics. 30% English instruction.
- Lv.5 (Advanced): Nuance, humor, slang, regional dialects, full Malay conversation. 10% English instruction.

## Anti-patterns
- Don't teach grammar rules before vocabulary — vocabulary first, grammar emerges from patterns
- Don't use romanized pronunciation guides excessively — Malay spelling is mostly phonetic
- Don't compare Malay to Indonesian differences at beginner level — it confuses
- Don't teach written/formal Malay first — spoken/conversational is more useful and motivating
